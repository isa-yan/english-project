Indoctrination from Childhood

This issue is always present in North Korean society. With indoctrination beginning in childhood, all the characters are taught to love the Kim dynasty. To the average DPRK citizen, Kim Il-sung and Kim Jong-il are godlike figures—perfect in every way. They're depicted as the perfect soldier, as a father figure to the whole nation, and as incredibly talented leaders—fighting against a terrible force: the US. Even our main character, Mi-ran, believed in this propaganda, she "felt she was quite lucky to have been born in North Korea under the loving care of the fatherly leader (Nothing to Envy, 12-13)."

North Korea vs Them

In North Korea, the government also uses the us-vs-them mindset to encourage nationalism and devotion towards the nation and their cause. By spreading media depicting American soldiers as terrible and beastly figures; spreading songs singing about destroying the US in honor of the communist spirit, the DPRK succeeded in indoctrinating their citizens at an early age to hate the "imperialist capitalists", instead encouraging people to embrace the "communist spirit".

Mrs. Song — A True Believer

Characters in the book, such as Mrs. Song believed strongly in this propaganda. As one of the "true believers", she idolized Kim Il-sung like the regime taught her to. She did everything she could to express her loyalty to the regime and its leaders—trying to maintain her image as the model citizen every moment she could. This same unwavering fealty wasn't paid back, however. Her husband, mother-in-law, and son ended up dying in the 1990s famine. Even "well past the point when it should have been obvious that the system had failed her, she remained unwavering in her faith. “I lived only for Marshal Kim Il-sung and for the fatherland. I never had a thought otherwise,” she told me the first time we met." (Nothing to Envy, 39) Mrs. Song based her whole life around the worker's party, demonstrating just how strong of an influence the regime can have over a person's beliefs, their hopes, and their entire way of life. Mrs. Song truly believed in the government's narrative, it reshaped her personality, her actions, and the entire course of her life.
