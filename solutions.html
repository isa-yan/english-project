Facebook and labelling

Some companies such as Facebook enacted policies to label media as state-controlled in an effort to combat foreign influence/narratives spreading outside said countries. When the labels are noticed, users are seen to engage with the content less, as compared to content without the label. Since users were able to discern the origin of the media, it helped to discourage trust in said sources—achieving Facebook’s goal of cutting out manipulative content.

Bringing light into the dark

Organizations, such as Reporters Without Borders, or Journal of Democracy have worked in effort to document behaviors leaders don’t want them to witness. Whether that be documenting human rights violations, spreading articles contrary to the desired narrative, or simply reporting on the prohibition of media, these organizations succeed in bringing attention to leaders using manipulation tactics on their followers. This works by bringing public scrutiny—often the most effective tool in discouraging engagement (Reporters Without Borders). However, in societies where media is highly scrutinized and scrubbed clean before it’s allowed to reach the populace, this often doesn’t do much to inform the citizens.

A North Korea specific solution:

Efforts have been made on the South Korean side of the border to spread leaflets and other such information into the DPRK (Tong-hyung). Whether that be through balloons, or other such methods, many anti-DPRK organizations have tried to spread awareness of their cause even through the tightly guarded North Korean information wall. In 2023, this was made even easier by the South Korean supreme court striking down a law banning anti-Pyongyang leaflets. It is unknown how useful these methods are, since there is still an incredibly strict guard system that blocks out information from entering and leaving North Korea. Due to this, it is almost impossible to tell if there have been people convinced by the leaflets at all.

Media Literacy as a Solution

Another method that has been used by governments and online organizations is the increasing call for media literacy among the generations of today. Many school districts, such as Fairfax County, have been adding Media Literacy as a must-know for students—trying to teach students how to recognize bias and detect fallacies in writing (“Digital Citizenship Student Curriculum”). In an era where the truth is becoming harder and harder to find, media literacy becomes a way to develop the critical thinking skills necessary to recognize manipulated news. By teaching people how to think for themselves; learning how to categorize and discern information—media literacy makes it so that people can independently determine if the us-vs-them mentality is present in writing, or if a particular piece of media had its content edited to fit a message.

Media literacy!

Why?

The most effective plan is the media literacy plan. This plan is simply the best investment to help people recognize media bias and resist manipulation. It addresses all manipulation tactics and it helps build long-term resistance towards propaganda. Media literacy is just another way to teach critical thinking—an important skill that helps students “evaluate the truth, verify sources” and more. As compared to the other plans, this plan is the best at teaching people themselves the innate ability to judge something with their best judgement. According to the New York State Bar Association—one of the most prestigious law organizations in the US, “ The theme of the New York State Bar Association’s second Civics Convocation, to be held in October 2025, will be media literacy” (David). Seeing how even the NYSBA has prioritized media literacy, it only serves to further promote the importance of said topic. According to Media Literacy Now, 80% of teachers agreed upon the essential nature of media literacy. The Pew Research Center also offers an insight into student’s views as well, showing that around 68% of high school students don’t believe they’re equipped to evaluate sources themselves (David). From this we can see how fundamentally essential media literacy is. In order to protect our students, to teach people the skills needed for survival in a modern society—this is simply the best way. Knowledge is power, and wisdom is key. If everyone knew media literacy, perhaps we could save people from falling into the grips of the untruth. Escaping propaganda and falsehoods, barging their own way and determining their own truth from sources they deem right. This is what democracy stands for and this is what exemplifies a person with agency. With power.
