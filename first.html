Who uses the "us-vs-them" mentality?

Totalitarian leaders often use the “us vs. them” mentality, using language that separates the “us” from the outsiders, or the so-called “them”. Trying to make “us” seem like they have all the answers, while the “them” are foolish—worse than “us”. They use special jargon to inspire intrigue and create a sense of sacredness. This applies to the others too. Famous cult leaders such as Jones and Applewhite use vocab such as “Luciferian program” and more to designate outsiders as lesser. In today’s society, this happens with leaders in Slovakia, Italy, and the US as well. Think of Trump’s “fake news”, “crooked Hillary”, etc to perpetuate the us vs. them mentality (Cultish p.72-75). This is a good example of how political leaders use this method of thinking to gather support and direct anger towards their enemies. In this particular case, the “fake news” saying spread especially quickly through social media, now especially prevalent in modern society.

Bikram Yoga vs Hot Yoga

Bikram Choudhury (founder of Bikram Yoga) built a devoted following, partly due to his use of the “us vs them” mentality. Treating his philosophy as the only true “truth” for Hot Yoga and as the path to enlightenment, he built a fiercely loyal following. His Yoga involved a strict regimen of 26 poses, with devotees taught it was the only “real” form of Yoga. Along with this mentality, he also performed a form of “love bombing”: switching between proclamations of love to insults, he was seen as a “big child” by his followers. His followers were conditioned to believe every statement of his; taught to trust him more than they did their own pain, eventually leading to complete and total devotion.

How states use media to promote the us-vs-them mentality

In countries such as Russia, China, and Vietnam, the government uses state controlled media—spreading official narratives to help engrain regime control in authoritarian societies. Often spreading messages of isolationism, self-reliance, and anti US sentiment (often showing the US as "the others" trying to destroy their country or as a direct competitor). These governments often use manipulation tactics such as hiding disruptive politics and information from the general public and other key audiences to promote their own opinions (Walker and Orttung). In the DPRK today, the regime controls the flow of information and prohibits journalism that doesn’t promote the nation’s narrative. The leader of the DPRK today—Kim Jong-un, is the leader of the regime. Under his leadership, he makes sure that the media only reports information beneficial to the regime; doing this via repression, censorship, and propaganda (“North Korea”). In addition, the DPRK, through extensive indoctrination—engraving a deep sense of a paranoia and nationalism in the average citizen, Kim Il-sung was able to instill the belief that North Korea was superior to outside forces among the populace. Using the same us-vs-them mentality, the country is still extremely isolationist and resistant to outside influence to this day (“CultureGrams Online Database | World Edition: General Attitudes for North Korea”).
